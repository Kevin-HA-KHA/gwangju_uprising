The Deconstruction of a Myth

Testimonies and the Duty of Remembrance of the Gwangju Uprising

Note

The Gwangju Uprising is a significant historical event in the struggle for democracy in South Korea. The film "A Taxi Driver," which has become a gateway to learning more, played an important role in popularizing this story among the general public, especially young generations who knew little about this conflict. The film highlights the presence and role of the German journalist Jürgen Hinzpeter, but it also conveys a mystification of his presence, giving the impression that he was the only foreign journalist covering the events.

Jürgen Hinzpeter, the only foreign journalist on the scene?

“Who is Hinzpeter ?”

This was the reaction of French photographer Patrick Chauvel when questioned about the presence of other foreigners during the uprising.

Contrary to the idea conveyed by the film, Hinzpeter was not the only foreigner in Gwangju. In reality, at least two French photographers, Patrick Chauvel and François Lochon, were on site. These photographers played an important role in documenting the events and providing visual evidence of the brutality of the repression. Chauvel explains: “They [the soldiers] had asked us to go to a hotel and not leave. We never went there.”

“We positioned ourselves on the roof of the building right across from the city hall. I told Lochon that this is where the fighting would take place.”

Patrick Chauvel and François Lochon captured poignant images of the uprising. Their work is particularly valuable because it offers a different perspective from that of Hinzpeter. Their photos are powerful visual testimonies of the violence and courage of the citizens of Gwangju.

The place of archive images in the work of remembrance

Focus

Images play a central role in transmitting the memory of events. This is also true of the Gwangju uprising. But what role do they play in the transmission of this event, which is still the subject of debate in Korean society?

1980년 5월 광주에서
May 1980 at Gwangju

History

The importance of these archival images cannot be ignored, as they play a key role in various aspects of the memory work to which the 5-18 Foundation is committed. They are visual evidence of Gwangju's militancy, uprisings and violence. However, it is necessary to qualify this statement, as these images, although witnesses, cannot be completely objective, as their perception of events can change depending on the photographer's bias. Even if we wish to remain objective, they remain tainted by the feelings of the person who took them at that moment. So there's always an element of subjectivity in their interpretation. Right from the start of the uprising, journalists had a great deal of freedom to come and go in the field.

This made it possible to document the events, but the situation did not last. Indeed, unrestricted access to these images, which made it possible to document and give a high profile to the situation in Gwangju, subsequently proved problematic for the Korean government, whose authority was portrayed as highly repressive. As a result, images of Gwangju were inaccessible for a long time, as if to prevent the memory of this event from being passed on. This demonstrates the central role of images in the transmission of memory. Restricting access to them is tantamount to refusing to pass on and raise awareness of a part of the history of Gwangju.

Going further

When the restrictions on images of the Gwangju uprising were lifted, it became necessary to begin the work of identifying, collecting and preserving these images, in order to disseminate them and raise awareness of the event.This work is carried out on a daily basis by the 5-18 Foundation, founded in 1994.Its aim is to promote the spirit of May 18 in order to strengthen democracy in South Korea and respect the sacrifice of the Gwangju victims.It has a large bank of archival images of the uprising to help it carry out its missions and document the people.These images are also used for official commemorations on Gwangju Uprising Memorial Day.Here, they are used as a reminder of the importance of the struggle for democracy.Although well-documented, the Gwangju uprising still divides some people in South Korea, and a minority view the event as a fantasy.

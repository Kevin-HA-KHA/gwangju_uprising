The archivist's job

Focus

During our stay in Gwangju, we had the opportunity to interview an archivist from the May 18 Foundation. This encounter allowed us to discover this little-known profession through a person deeply committed to preserving the history of the Gwangju Uprising, giving us insight into the daily life of an archivist.

1980년 5월 광주에서
May 1980 at Gwangju

History

The role of an archivist is central to the preservation and transmission of memory. In the case of the Gwangju uprising, the archivist we interviewed is dedicated to collecting, preserving and making available historical documents, eyewitness accounts and photographs of the event. Thanks to the archivist's meticulous work, the public has access to a rich source of information, which remains essential for understanding this part of South Korean history. An archivist's work is not limited to the technical aspect; he or she also plays a key role in educating the public, organizing exhibitions and commemorative events that raise awareness of the importance of remembering the Gwangju uprising.

To get a better idea of what an archivist does, we asked our interviewee to explain in more detail her day-to-day work and the challenges she faces. Going the extra mile in this profession means not only managing a large volume of historical documents, but also taking into account ethical and moral issues that can sometimes be tricky. For example, how can we preserve the authenticity of documents while respecting the confidentiality of those involved? In addition, new technologies now play a central role in an archivist's work. The digitization of archives, for example, not only preserves physical documents against the wear and tear of time, but also makes these resources accessible to a global audience via online platforms.

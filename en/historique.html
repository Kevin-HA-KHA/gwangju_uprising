The Students of Gwangju Organize

The students of Gwangju organize to protest against these measures and the arrest of Kim Dae-jung, a democratic leader. On May 18, 1980, which marks the first day of the uprising, military forces brutally suppress the protests, triggering a massive reaction from the population. As a result, the residents of Gwangju support the students, amplifying the revolt. These combined factors led to the outbreak of the Gwangju Uprising as we know it today.

The Beginnings of the Uprising

Several elements led to the Gwangju Uprising.

Since 1961, South Korea had been under the dictatorship of Park Chung-hee, marked by repression and the suppression of civil liberties. In 1979, Park was assassinated, creating political instability. Subsequently, in December 1979, General Chun Doo-hwan seized power through a military coup. Then, in May 1980, Chun Doo-hwan extended martial law, closed universities, banned gatherings, and arrested opposition leaders.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

In Gwangju, 200 Students Gather to Protest Peacefully

In response to the closure of Chonnam University, around 200 students gather to protest peacefully against the closure of their university and the arrest of Kim Dae-jung, an influential pro-democracy leader. Special forces (paratroopers from the 7th Division) intervene brutally, using batons and tear gas to disperse the crowd. Many students are beaten and arrested, causing widespread outrage among the residents.

Start of the Uprising

After the assassination of President Park Chung-hee in October 1979, General Chun Doo-hwan seized power through a military coup in December 1979. Martial law was extended throughout the country. Under the extended martial law, political and social tensions were high. General Chun Doo-hwan, having consolidated his power, imposed strict measures, including the closure of universities.

Escalating Violence

The clashes between protesters and security forces intensify. Soldiers fire live ammunition, killing and injuring several protesters. The military's violence further galvanizes the protests.

Larger Protests

In response to the military violence, the protests escalate. The number of protesters grows to several thousand, including students and ordinary citizens.

The slogans call for the end of martial law and the resignation of Chun Doo-hwan.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Arming the Protesters.

Protesters manage to seize military vehicles and light weapons, intensifying the resistance. Government buildings and police stations are attacked and set on fire.

The number of deaths and injuries rises significantly. Hospitals are overwhelmed and lack resources to treat all the wounded.

Violent Clashes

Protesters begin using stones, bottles, and Molotov cocktails against the military forces. Scenes of violence multiply throughout the city.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Citizen Committees.

Self-management committees form to organize the city's defense, distribute supplies, and maintain order. Solidarity among the residents is strong.

Army gunfire kills many protesters, further exacerbating the population's anger.

Security Forces' Retreat

Facing intense resistance, security forces withdraw from several areas of the city. Protesters take control of major buildings and infrastructure.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Communication.

Protesters use local radio stations to broadcast information and call for outside help. However, communications are often jammed by the government.

Popular Support

The population of Gwangju shows massive support for the protesters, providing food, blood for transfusions, and other forms of aid. Citizen committees continue to manage the city.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Maintaining Order

Despite negotiation attempts, citizen committees continue to manage the city and prepare for defense in case of a new military assault.

Discussions

Some clergy members, academics, and citizens attempt to negotiate with the military authorities for a peaceful resolution. The protesters demand the abolition of martial law and the release of political prisoners.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Military Preparation

Military forces regroup around Gwangju, ready to launch a massive attack to retake the city.

Ultimatum

Chun Doo-hwan's government issues an ultimatum to the protesters, ordering them to surrender by midnight. The protesters refuse, determined to continue their struggle.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Numerous Victims

The toll is heavy: hundreds of protesters are killed or injured. Hundreds more are arrested. There are also reports of rape, sexual assaults, and torture inflicted by the military.

Final Assault

In the early hours of May 27, elite troops of the South Korean army launch a major offensive against the protesters' positions. The assault is brutal and swift.

The military troops use armored vehicles and heavy weapons. Fierce fighting occurs, but the protesters are quickly overwhelmed by the military's superiority.

Title / Caption / Photo Credits

Political Consequences

The Gwangju Uprising becomes a powerful symbol of resistance against authoritarianism in South Korea. In 1987, popular pressure finally leads to democratic reforms. In 1997, President Kim Dae-jung, a former political prisoner, declares May 18 as a national commemoration day.

The Gwangju Uprising is now commemorated as a key moment in the struggle for democracy and civil rights in South Korea. Despite the heavy toll, it is seen today as a general symbol of hope in the fight for human freedoms.

Continued Repression

The government conducts a severe crackdown on dissidents and anyone suspected of participating in the uprising. Media censorship is intensified to control information about the events in Gwangju.

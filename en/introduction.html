The Duty of Memory and Truth

The May 18 Memorial Foundation, Guardian of History and Defender of Human Rights

Gwangju May 1st 2024

History

The May 18 Foundation was established to perpetuate the spirit of struggle and solidarity of the Gwangju Uprising. This organization also contributes to promoting and protecting democracy and human rights, both regionally and internationally, notably through the Gwangju Prize for Human Rights. Since the year 2000, this prize has recognized significant contributions to the promotion of human rights, democracy, and peace through the actions of its laureates.

After periods of repression, civil war, or dictatorship, many countries seek to establish the truth to foster acceptance, reconstruction, and reconciliation. The National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons in Argentina (1983) and the Truth and Reconciliation Commission in South Africa (1995) are pioneering examples. These commissions, composed of several members and chaired by a leader, organize hearings to gather victims' testimonies and review official documents in order to establish the truth transparently. South Korea also established such a commission in 2005 to investigate human rights violations that occurred during the Korean War and under the subsequent authoritarian regimes.

Going further

Many nations that have experienced periods of repression, political unrest, civil wars, dictatorships, or genocides seek to restore the truth. This pursuit of historical restoration is a first step toward acceptance, reconstruction, and reconciliation of a people with their past. In 1983, in Argentina, the first such effort in the world was initiated. The National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons was established after the military dictatorship to investigate forced disappearances and human rights violations committed during that period. The term "Truth and Reconciliation Commission" first emerged in South Africa. Established in 1995, the TRC aimed to foster national reconciliation, allowing victims of apartheid (a system of segregation) to share their stories.

A Truth and Reconciliation Commission is composed of several members and a chairperson. It holds numerous hearings where victims can testify. In addition to testimonies, the commission relies on official documents to establish the truth in a transparent manner. In South Korea, such a commission was established on December 1, 2005, as part of the government. It aims to shed light on the numerous human rights violations that occurred during the Korean War and under the various authoritarian regimes that followed.

The Duty of Memory and Truth

The May 18 Memorial Foundation, Guardian of History and Defender of Human Rights

Gwangju May 1st 2024

History

The May 18 Memorial Foundation was created to develop our nation by remembering the spirit of struggle and solidarity of the May 18 uprising. The organization also contributes to promoting and protecting democracy and human rights at both regional and international levels through the Gwangju Prize for Human Rights, which has been awarded since 2000 to recognize contributions to the promotion of human rights, democracy, and peace through their work.

After periods of repression, civil war, or dictatorship, many countries seek to establish the truth to foster acceptance, reconstruction, and reconciliation. The National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons in Argentina (1983) and the Truth and Reconciliation Commission in South Africa (1995) are pioneering examples. These commissions, composed of several members and a chairperson, hold hearings to collect testimonies from victims and examine official documents to establish the truth transparently. South Korea also established such a commission in 2005 to investigate human rights violations during the Korean War and the subsequent authoritarian regimes.

Going Further

Many nations that have gone through periods of repression, political turmoil, civil wars, dictatorships, or genocides seek to establish the truth. This quest to restore history is a first step toward acceptance, reconstruction, and reconciliation of a people with their history. In 1983, in Argentina, for the first time in the world, the path to healing was initiated. The National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons was established after the military dictatorship to investigate forced disappearances and human rights violations committed during that period. It was in South Africa that the now globally recognized term "Truth and Reconciliation Commission" first appeared. Established in 1995, the TRC sought to promote national reconciliation, allowing victims of apartheid (a system of segregation) to tell their stories.

A Truth and Reconciliation Commission is composed of several members and a chairperson. It holds numerous hearings where victims can testify. In addition to testimonies, the commission relies on official documents to transparently establish the truth. In South Korea, this commission was established on December 1, 2005, as an integral part of the government. It aims to shed light on the multiple human rights violations during the Korean War, as well as the various authoritarian regimes that followed.
